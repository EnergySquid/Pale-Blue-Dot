Climate Change

​Parts Per Million of CO2​ in Our Atmosphere
Climate change is real. All the science, conducted by reputable scientists, says that climate change is affecting our planet in highly negative ways. Averaged over all land and ocean surfaces, temperatures warmed roughly 1.53°F (0.85ºC) from 1880 to 2012. This may not seem like much, but small variations in average global temperatures can have very large effects. About 20% of the ice in the ice caps were on the verge of melting in 1979. Today, all 20% of this ice has melted, as shown by NASA's satellites (right). If another 20% of our ice melts in the next 30 years, that would be 1.8 million square kilometers of our present 2.6 million square kilometers of ice.

​Carbon Dioxide (CO2) is a greenhouse gas released through the burning of fossil fuels. Since the industrial revolution, CO2 levels have increased by 40%. ​
If our climate was allowed to warm by only 2°C, it could have disastrous effects. Many scientists believe this is the ​tipping point—the point where our climate would start to change all on its own, whether humans continued to release greenhouse gasses or not. This would mean that within a century or so, most costal cities would have to be abandoned, and hurricanes would become more common and more powerful. Other natural disasters would become more common and more devastating including more frequent powerful tornados, severe flooding, and severe drought (think California).
