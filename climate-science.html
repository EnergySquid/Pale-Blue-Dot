The Greenhouse Effect

Credit: ereidmil_sfdfdsf on ereidmiller.com
The greenhouse effect refers to the heat transfer between a planet, its sun, and space. For Earth, the energy comes from the sun, mostly in the form of visible light (740 nm to 380 nm). The atmosphere is mostly transparent in visible light, so light passes straight through. The light hits the surface of the Earth and is absorbed by land, the ocean, and pretty much anything that isn't a mirror. The atoms of this non-mirror absorb the energy from the light. Some of this energy is translated into heat. Anything that is above absolute zero emits some kind of electromagnetic radiation. For objects on earth, this tends to be in the infrared spectrum. The main components of the atmosphere, nitrogen, oxygen, and argon, are invisible in infrared as well as visible light. If the atmosphere was composed of only these three gasses, all the heat in the form of infrared radiation would be able to escape the atmosphere unimpeded and into space. If this were the case, the Earth would be approximately 33°C (91°F) cooler than it is today, which would put the average global temperature at roughly -18°C (0°F), which would not be good for life on earth. This is where Carbon Dioxide (CO2) comes into play. Unlike the other three gasses previously mentioned, CO2 is not invisible to infrared radiation. When hit by an infrared photon, CO2 absorbs and re-emits the photon in a random direction. Some of the time, the photon is just shot back up into space, but other times, it's shot straight back at the ground.
Credit: NASA
Greenhouse gasses can be thought of as blankets in the fall. Without one, you'll be too cold. But if you have too many, you'll become an oven. This is exactly whats happening to Earth. We're adding blankets every day, and less and less of the heat Earth receives can escape into space. We see the worst case scenario in our next door neighbor, Venus. The Venusian atmosphere at the surface has 90 times the pressure of Earth's atmosphere at sea level, and has surface temperatures of over 470°C (880°F). Its atmosphere is made of mostly comprised of CO2. You might think, "this is just because Venus is closer to the Sun," but you'd be wrong. By that logic, Mercury, the closest planet to the Sun, would have the highest surface temperatures of the planets. This is not the case. While the temperatures can reach a scorching 430°C (800°F), this is the high, and without an atmosphere to hold onto the heat at night, the temperatures on the night side can be as low as -180°C (-290°F).
Alright, so increased CO2 levels cause temperatures to rise. How do we know that it's happening on Earth, and how do we know humans are the cause of it?
We know it's happening on Earth for some pretty simple reasons. We've been directly measuring CO2​ in the atmosphere since 1958 at the Mauna Loa Observatory in Hawaii. Since March 1958, CO2​ levels have risen from 315.71 Parts Per Million (PPM) to 406.09 PPM on April 4, 2016. That's an increase of 88.31 PPM over only 58 years. That may not seem like much, but many climate scientists have estimated that 400 PPM is the point of no return that could be the tipping point of our climate.
We know we are the cause for a more complex reason, and it requires a little bit of knowledge of the subatomic world. There's more than just one kind of carbon atom. The most common is called carbon twelve, it has the twelve because it has an atomic mass of 12. That is, 6 protons and 6 neutrons. Giving it an atomic mass of 12 (6 + 6 = 12). Carbon 13 is similar, it has 6 protons, that's what makes it carbon, but it has 7 neutrons. This is called an isotope. In fossil fuels, there is less carbon 13 than in the atmosphere. If you added all that carbon into the atmosphere, you'd expect the total concentration of carbon 13 in the atmosphere relative to carbon 12 to decrease. And using some fancy math, you can calculate exactly how large that change will be given an amount of fossil fuels burned. Scientists have run the numbers, and they match up.

All of this evidence tells us that:
1. CO2​ causes warming
2. The level of CO2​ in our atmosphere is increasing
3. The new CO2​ is from the burning of fossil fuels
